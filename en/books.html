three sentence book reviews



This is a beautifully written "America is more racist than you think" book, with all the strengths and failures of the genre: lovely prose, strained analogies, a firm grasp of history, and no vision for the future. For whatever reason it gives no consideration to Asian, Latin, or Native Americans, glancing at America's racial hierarchy only at its top and bottom. Not worth your time if you've read this kind of thing before.


Carol Dweck has done decades of research showing people who view skills as the result of effort are more successful than people who view skills as innate. Apparently these are some of the most bulletproof, replicable studies out there. But she's way better at psychology than writing for a popular audience.


The title here is a double entendre: the book concerns adults acting like kids and a pedophile who moves into their neighborhood. Not much really happens. But Perotta's vivid characters and punchy dialogue carry the Desparate Housewives style plot threads.



Parents aren't always upfront about how unpleasant babies can be. Mike Birbiglia is, and he's funny about it. Very honest and kind of redeeming at the end.


There seems to have been little internal struggle in Benjamin Franklin's life. He decided on some values when he was young, applied them to his daily life, and then accomplished a lot. Though incomplete and written in bits and pieces, this autobiography is a fascinating portrait of an American ubermensch.


Neil Postman's cranky but visionary screed argues entertainment and distraction hollow out culture and politics. Written thirty years before Twitter and a reality TV president, Postman's claims are prophetic. The book's hectoring tone does detract from its insight.



If the book were called "Are Prisons Bad?" it would be a success—Davis highlights various problems with prisons in well-researched, accessible prose. But it's called "Are Prisons Obsolete?" and it fails as an abolitionist text because Davis' alternative to prisons is hazily-defined socialism and the end of crime. Davis puts the question "What about rapists and murderers?" in scare quotes, but never actually answers it.


The premise of the book makes sense: we should have broad utopian goals and avoid getting mired in technocratic iteration. But Bregman argues for basic income, open borders, and shorter work weeks with no consideration for counterarguments, and no reason to believe these are utopian ideals rather than policies governments should adopt now. He oversells his argument, but Bregman's broader point about the value of utopian thinking is sound and important.


President Obama does two things well here: he offers a good sense of what it's like to be president and he's an excellent apologist for the Obama Administration. While a bit self-serving at times (not quite The Last Dance-bad but approaching it), this is a well-written, fascinating memoir. Anyone interested in politics will love reliving moments like Henry Louis Gates' arrest or the Deepwater Horizon tragedy from the White House's perspective.



Studwell discusses some rich countries and some poor countries in Asia that were at about the same starting point following the Second World War. The rich countries did three things the poor ones didn't according to Studwell: land reform to promote small-scale agriculture, tempering large manufacturing subsidies with what he calls "export discipline" (the practice of only subsidizing globally competitive companies), and a "tight leash" on the financial system to keep capital flowing into agriculture and manufacturing. His argument is clear, persuasive, and rich with examples.


I live about 35 minutes away from Yamhill, Oregon and was excited to read Nicholas Kristof write about the undoing of his hometown. But only about a third of this book takes place in Yamhill—the rest wanders through random places in the US to present unoriginal liberal takes on various social issues. I agree with the arguments here, but the book is shallow and disappointing.


David Brooks' life advice is so much better than his political commentary. He describes two stages of life: a first mountain where one climbs toward money, power, and worldly success; and then a second mountain where one ascends toward higher values. Brooks shows a strong grasp of the moral pulse of America, though his advice is sort of geared at everyone and no-one.



Writing before smartphones were ubiquitous, Carr explains how the internet changes our thinking. The book is deeply researched, discussing neuroplasticity and how brains have historically adapted to new communication technologies. Carr expresses the urgency of not misusing the web without histrionics.


Crane uses an HIV clinic in Uganda to make an alarming argument: global health initiatives tend to "create and rely upon" the inequalities they are designed to fix. I wasn't convinced. While there's an enormous power difference between the Ugandan patients and Western doctors, the injustices that come out of it seem minor relative to work being done on HIV.


Lindsey and Pluckrose bring clarity to inscrutable trends in academia, presenting the most lucid explanations of "postmodernism" and "critical theory" I've seen. But the book assumes more social justice than exists in the world, particularly with regard to gay and transgender people. As it becomes more polemic toward the end, it sometimes treats activism as unnecessary.



This is the best book I've read in a long time. Michelle Alexander left a gaping hole in The New Jim Crow by not discussing violent crime or initial black support for mass incarceration. Forman plugs it with precision and righteousness in this detailed exploration of 1970s carceral politics.


Sered's case for restorative justice responses to violent crime starts strong then falters. Her explanations for why prison doesn't work are detailed and persuasive, but she follows them up with weak, anecdote-driven arguments for restorative justice alternatives. That this book makes no mention of the violent crime's dramatic fall over the past thirty years is telling—Sered is here for ideological musings, not solutions.


Who would look at American politics and think we need to define "racism" in such a way that tax cuts are racist? Why not just come up with a new word? Adam Serwer said history books make the best antiracist texts—they're certainly more enlightening that watching Kendi play semantic games.



Not good. White fragility is a useful concept, but it's packaged really poorly here and the term is snarky for no reason. I wrote more about it here.


Ashley Jardina's explores years of survey data regarding people who strongly identify as white. By looking at white identity through the lens of in-group affection rather than out-group hostility, she makes some interesting insights. For example, white identifiers tend to be indifferent to affirmative action.


This is the platonic ideal of the interview with Trump supporters in a Midwestern diner. Katherine Cramer, a native Wisconsinite, published this book months before the 2016 election after working on it for almost a decade. Her conversations tease out the dynamics of rural resentment—it's more subtle than racism or economic anxiety.



Martin Luther King's writing is just as beautiful as his speaking. In this, his last book before his death, he paints a picture of a second phase of the Civil Rights Movement that moves beyond treating black people with decency and strives for material equality. He was a minister and not an economist, so I'm not sure about the viability of his UBI schemes, but his thoughts on the activism of his time are as relevant as ever.


In what Achens and Bartels call the "folk theory of democracy," democracy works because government is responsive to informed citizens voting in their self interest. Since voting behavior is largely determined by identity and other factors unrelated to the effectiveness of elected officials, Achens and Bartels argue the folk theory of democracy is a bad way to look at things. The path forward from this conclusion is hazy, but their assertion that group identities are central seems correct.


This book is so unfair! It's page after page of two Afghan women facing unbearable sexism. Khaled Hosseini writes sensitively about how in response to oppression they at times turn against one another and at others band together.



The "I got fired by Trump and need to cash out while I'm relevant" genre of political memoir is rife with dumb palace intrigue, self-righteousness, and hurried writing. Happily Preet Bharara's book mostly sidesteps these pitfalls. Bharara writes candidly about his time running the country's most famous prosecutor's office, with only the occasional low-effort, anti-Trump bromide.


This mostly faithful translation of the opening book in A Series of Unfortunate Events captures Lemony Snicket's tone well. Some of the wordplay is missing (e.g., Al Funcoot is an anagram of Count Olaf, while Alfred Tourtebuse n'a rien à voir avec le comte Olaf). But on the whole, this is a great read for people learning French, especially if you read these books as a kid.


Klein sees our country as no more divided than it was in the middle of the last century. Segregationist Southern Democrats were not aligned with other Democrats, preventing the country's divisions from neatly mapping onto its politics the way it does today. Klein's conclusion that we were always polarized is a bit too cute.



The recent, historic drop in violent crime has had as great a public health benefit for young black men as would the eradication of obesity. Sharkey suggests methods for reversing the recent uptick in crime and continuing the broader, positive trend without the sort of aggressive policing and prosecution that harms minority communities. His lucid writing comes from a place of careful study rather than ideology.


When Bloom says empathy, he doesn't mean compassion or even imagining what someone else is experiencing. He means literally feeling what another person feels. While the book builds on a nit-picky definition, he argues well that our ability to relate to others is too biased and unreliable to form the basis of our morals.


Atwood builds a detailed world that unlike many dystopias becomes more plausible the further one reads. There's not much subtlety at first, and the book kind of beats you over the head with its message. But as the power dynamics come into focus and Offred's character grows in dimension, the book becomes more interesting.



Reading this made me feel like I was going to be asked to donate to something. Mary Robinson presents hopeful stories of people fighting climate change. I like the framing of climate change as a moral issue and a feminist issue, but it's hard not to roll one's eyes at writing that seems geared toward the sort of people who attend international conferences.


David Graeber's viral 2013 essay "On the phenomenon of bullshit jobs" exposed a number of meaningless jobs that could disappear overnight with no effect on the world (or even a positive effect). Released five years later, this book adds a more detailed bullshit job typology and bullshit job testimonies from people suffering the spiritual violence of knowing their work is entirely pointless. The book is funny and incisive, though overstated at times.


This book is full of ideas. I sprinted through it and felt like I missed a lot of interesting thoughts on art and ethics. It's a fun story, but definitely not a light read.



Most progressive critics of inequality support meritocracy; the system is seen as unjust because rich people cheat or because minorities are discriminated against. But Markovits argues meritocracy is itself the problem, restructuring the economy in ways that hollow out the middle class and creating an ever-narrowing band of meritocratic elites working inhumane hours. Though his alternatives are underdeveloped, he makes a thorough and convincing case for reconsidering standard redistributive fixes to inequality.


This book is a good introduction to socialism, but there's a jarring tonal shift after the first chapter. It starts with a whimsical thought experiment exploring the downsides of capitalism and the dream of a truly egalitarian socialist society from the perspective of a worker in a pasta sauce factory run by Bon Jovi's family. Then the book takes a sharp turn to some dry history, with lines like "The seeds of what would become Second International Marxism were apparent in the SDAP program, but when the party merged with the ADAV six years later to form the twenty-thousand-strong Socialist Workers' Party of Germany (SAP), the new organization's founding document had a distinct Lassallean flavor."


Heather MacDonald argues unwarranted skepticism toward police puts minorities at risk by making cops less proactive. But by not discussing drug law enforcement beyond the crack/cocaine disparity and frequently making use of inflammatory anecdotes, MacDonald writes for people who already agree with her. Where The New Jim Crow ignores disparities in violent crime rates, The War on Cops ignores equivalent drug usage rates, and its arguments suffer as a result.



This book is an excellent primer on Rwandan history and politics. Joseph Sebarenzi is fair and played a key role in his country's story. It's a very readable memoir that moves at a good clip.


This book drags. The premise is interesting: in a small Mississippi town in the late 80's, a black man kills the white men who raped his daughter and faces trial. But it's clear this is John Grisham's first crack at a novel, with endless hearings and motions and arraignments and the stuff they cut out off Law and Order.


The first three chapters are great: bell hooks presents a clear definition of love and describes how most people don’t experience it. But then she abandons that definition and starts writing crazy shit like “if all public policy was created in the spirit of love, we would not have to worry about unemployment, homelessness, schools failing to teach children, or addiction.” In hooks’ world, everything bad is because of mass media and the patriarchy, and it could all be fixed if we loved each other.



People of all races use drugs at about the same rates, but the War on Drugs and the resulting explosion in incarceration rates has disproportionately landed on minority communities. Michelle Alexander argues that mass incarceration is a system of racial control of the same scale and nature as slavery and Jim Crow. The absence of an extended discussion of elevated violent crime rates in minority communities and some tenuous historical allusions (she argues building prisons in rural areas boosts population and thus congressional representation in white majority areas in a manner reminiscent to the three-fifths compromise) detract from an otherwise compelling argument.


Camus' writing is clear and easy to read. He puts the main character in a bunch of awkward situations that seem to accumulate without cohering. But there's actually a pretty cool, straightforward philosophical idea being presented here (the Sparknotes is useful if it went over your head like it did mine).


The profanity is a gimmick, but the substance of the book is great. Mark Manson critiques consumerist, individualist culture in fresh ways. He offers effective tools for wading through it all without giving a fuck.



Coates writes beautifully. But he’s too cynical. Blackness doesn’t mean relentless struggle against oppression.


Leovy’s nonfiction account of a murder in L.A. reads like a detective novel. She cuts through the politics to present one way to reduce black-on-black violence: more thorough investigations. Very well-researched, very emotional, and deeply concerned with preserving black lives.


Very cute drawings. Lovely story. Probably deep, but over my head.



Barbara Ehrenreich is old enough to die. This book discusses her acceptance of that reality and her frustration with a health culture more intent on extending life than enjoying it. She makes strong arguments, but often digresses.


Eugenia Cheng has a gift for analogies. She makes research mathematics accessible with constant references to baking. It's like the Mm... Food of math texts.


Villani does a great job of showing how math is like poetry, and crucially, how math is not like poetry. It’s easy to get lost in important sounding vagaries when talking about math. But Villani is precise and develops a compelling analogy.



Finally! A clean description of populism: an aesthetic wrapper around any given ideology delineating a morally pure people and a corrupt elite. Mudde and Kaltwasser give detailed examples from around the globe to develop a clear definition.


Westover's insane childhood deserves a memoir, and she delivers. She describes her journey from a violent, cloistered religious family in rural Idaho who wouldn't let her attend school to a Ph.D from Cambridge. Lots of thoughtful reflections on family and education draw from her experience of the extremes of both.


Most political memoirs are unreadable, but this one is different. Michelle Obama chronicles her journey from the Southside of Chicago to the Ivy League to the White House with a relatable, human voice. My favorite parts were when she talked about the challenge of raising kids while her husband was a state senator and she was a professional woman.



Very readable — I think this was meant for workers while Capital was meant for intellectuals. But no thanks, Karl and Friedrich. I like property.


Feelings are bullshit, the "self" isn't a coherent idea, and you should probably meditate. Robert Wright presents these ideas from Buddhism and shows how they're supported by modern science. I'm convinced.


Malala's story humanizes the Swat Valley. Her descriptions of the weather, the people, and her daily struggle to get educated show a side of Pakistan we rarely see in depth in the West. She's a hero, and this book deserves all the praise it's received.



Sandra Uwiringiyimana's childhood was horrifying, with her seeing her sister die in conflict in Eastern Congo. But my favorite parts of the book were toward the end, when she discusses the difficult adjustment to life in the US. We often cut off stories when the protagonist makes it out of danger, but Sandra's descriptions of what happens after the credits roll lands well.


This book covers the history of translation software while managing to avoid getting too technical. Thierry Poibeau starts with rule-based translation and builds up to the deep learning models that power Google Translate. Great overview.


Equal parts dry and ideological, this book is a chore. Zinn's repetitiveness and leftist politics overpower his eye-opening nuggets of US history often ignored by textbooks. I tried reading it backward, from the Clinton years to Columbus chapter by chapter, which made it a little more interesting.



Scott Jurek is vegan. He also runs ultramarathons. This memoir discusses his eating and running, interspersed with recipes and full of insight into a simple life.


Cal Newport argues convincingly that passion is not a great thing to build a career around. Instead he advocates developing rare and valuable skills that give one the leverage needed to gain autonomy. Job satisfaction and job security come with being indispensable.


Paul Campos is a law professor who has repeatedly called law school a scam. The numbers are on his side: the job market for attorneys simply doesn't align with the cost of law school. His style can be a bit condescending, but the message is important for young people considering law school.



This graphic novel takes place in the corner of a living room. Every pane looks at the same spot, but varies across time, ranging from millions of years in the past to thousands of years in the future. Richard McGuire's creative exploration of the scale of our time on the planet shows what we're doing to the climate.


Racial dynamics in Portland are interesting because the city is very progressive, but also has an exclusionary past that has led to a tiny, fairly marginalized minority population. Renée Watson explores all this through the lens of a black high school student who buses across town to a fancy private school. She nails the feeling of being talked down to by racially insensitive (or racially oversensitive) liberals.


Many people frame this book as an airing of grievances, but while there's a small amount of griping, it's a pretty spot-on analysis of the 2016 election. Clinton takes responsibility for the things that were her fault and complains about the things that weren't. There were a lot of reasons she lost, and this book goes through them carefully using the best evidence available.



This book is not good. What First Nations people have experienced in Canada is horrifying. But the "white men came to steal our dreams" twist ending is such identitarian left nonsense.


Amy Chua’s parenting style dances on the edge of abuse. Forcing her kids to practice violin for 6 hours, calling them losers — this is crossing a line. The kids are grown and successful and say they wouldn’t want to have been raised any other way, but I think it’s Stockholm Syndrome.


Richard Reeves makes the case that the richest 20%, not 1% are the real villains of American capitalism. He describes how zoning laws and other legal mechanisms prevent upward mobility for the rest of the country. Seems about right.



This book is dark. The premise sounds a bit YA: a young girl founds a new religion in the middle of a climate disaster. But there's quite a bit of rape and gore, and Butler has a knack for nonchalant sentences that stop you in your tracks and make you realize how fucked up the situation is.


O'Neill is at times alarmist, but makes a compelling case that algorithmic thinking is often used to disguise value judgements. I totally agree with her, for example, that the US News and World Report college rankings are literally cancer. It's great to hear this argument coming from a mathematician too -- it would be probably less convincing coming from a social scientist.


The controversial section on race didn't seem all that controversial; it's plausible to me that racial differences in IQ have a genetic origin, and Murray and Herrnstein go no further than stating it's a possibility. Beyond that, the thesis of the book is provocative: meritocracy is creating an unintelligent underclass. It's a perspective on inequality I haven't heard before.



Douglas Hofstadter's 1979 classic Gödel, Escher, Bach is long and difficult. So in 2008, he re-explored his ambitious theory of cognition in a more streamlined, personal book. Some of the whimsy of GEB is traded for blunt emotionality; the loss of his wife plays a role in his thinking on consciousness, and he does not hide this fact from the reader.


Chimamanda Adichie writes beautifully as always. But there are too many named characters who hang out for a few chapters without doing anything interesting. The book meanders quite bit, but runs into many poignant moments along the way.


Tom Perotta has captured late 2010s suburban America in this book. Eve Fletcher sends her son off to college and develops a porn addiction. The way we interact with technology and the way activism is challenging certain norms and is neatly bound in this charming novel.



I think Ben Carson's politics are horrible to poor people, and his views of social movements seem pretty ahistorical (he calls Martin Luther King a conservative). But I love how he, a rare black doctor in 70s, responded when constantly called an orderly or a nurse. He would calmly correct the other person and watch their embarrassment make them rethink their assumptions -- today's call-out culture would have a lot to learn from that attitude.


This isn’t a how-to guide, but instead a novel written entirely in the second person. You escape poverty, fall in love, and get rich in some unnamed South Asian country. But at at what cost?


This is the really the last self-help book. Nothing I read it in it was new -- it was just an effective framing and synthesis of years of broad life advice I've heard. And now most of the time I hear broad life advice or self-help, I can peg it to one of the seven habits in this book.



The premise of this book is a little shoddy, though I might be taking it more seriously than it's meant to be taken. My understanding is that this book is like a very broad briefing for a would-be president, arming them with just enough scientific literacy to not do completely stupid things. The problem is that in covering so much in such little space, it mostly left my ahead as quickly as it entered.


This short story collection from Chimamanda Adichie is damn near perfect. At the end of every single story I was left wanting just a little more. She's so good at putting readers in the minds of characters.


Everyone I know who runs a lot has an opinion about this book. Christopher McDougall makes some arguments that may well be true, but aren't exactly peer-reviewed, namely that running shoes with too much cushions cause injuries and that human beings evolved to run long distances. His writing is engaging, and his descriptions of the Tarahumara are respectful.



Some really cool ideas about infinity lurk behind calculus. Many motivated students grind their way through integrating and differentiating without over finding the opportunity to step back and really think about what they're doing. Eugenia Cheng presents all the weirdness and wonder of infinity in accessible pop science form.


Shadi Hamid brings his expertise on the Arab world to bear on some sensitive questions. He argues that Islam is, in fact, distinct from other faiths in its political ambitions. His treatment of the Arab Spring and its aftermath is carefully argued.


Anjan Sundaram left a life of comfortable abstraction as a mathematician to become a reporter in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. His writing is sharp and immediate. I loved reading about him getting robbed and having a very difficult time adjusting -- there is no sugar-coating here.



This is simply not the book I was looking for, and I was deeply disappointed as a result. Sarah Bakewell is far more concerned with the lives and dramas of the philosophers mentioned than their ideas. I can see why some people would be into that, but I find existentialist ideas far more interesting than how they saved Heidegger's manuscripts during World War II.


Ayelet Waldman isn't particularly likable. For context, she fielded controversy (and publicity) by saying she loved her husband more than her kids on daytime TV. Her journey into micro-dosing LSD is interesting, but I found it difficult to feel like I was rooting for her.


Anjan Sundaram finished his master's degree in mathematics at Yale before setting off to the Democratic Republic of the Congo to become a reporter. His sharp turn to journalism in central Africa is the core of his first book, Stringer. This new book, about a crackdown on free speech in neighboring Rwanda, is more serious, more dire and more important for people to read.



Ian Bogost writes high-minded stories in The Atlantic about technology. This book is just as high-minded, and it's annoying. He could describe his interesting definition of play and his thoughts on incorporating play into everyday life without asides on Heideggerian intentionality.


Rachel Maddow's cable show is disappointingly partisan, especially since the 2016 election. This book however, offers an razor sharp critique of the growing gap between military and civilian life. There are cathartic digs at Reagan and the like that don't do much to advance the argument, but this book is smart and important.


David Brooks feels our culture has drifted away from character and has become overly concerned with worldly accomplishment. He draws from several biographies to build an idea of what a humble life of character looks like. It's well put together.



Milan Kundera paints rich, textured characters and throws them into revolutionary Prague in 1968. They have sex with each other, misunderstand each other and interact in unpredictable but genuine seeming ways. I love the sections titled "A Short Dictionary of Misunderstood Words" (or something like that I haven't read the book in a while), where Kundera shows how characters define words differently to show how they can't really get to know each other.


Benjamin Sáenz writes tenderly about family and identity. He knows how to write teenagers—their problems seem weighty but age appropriate. The fact that it won a Stonewall Book Award kind of ruin the twist.


Malcolm X speaks to a perspective on black struggle that seems more honest and less effective. He was right that nonviolence tended to mean defenselessness. He was wrong that it would get black people nowhere.



Cal Newport builds a great framework for accomplishing serious knowledge work. His suggestions are fairly radical (most notably deleting social media). I don't think most people are trying to do the sort of work this book is about, but for those who are, Newport's ideas are worth at the very least considering.


Black Africans and black Americans have very different histories and cultures. Wamba, the son of an African dad and American mom, tries to bridge the gap. I don’t think he succeeds.


I asked my librarian for a book about friendship and she recommended this one. But are Lennie and George even friends? Lennie just kind of puts up with George.



eCoNoMiC aNxIeTy: the book. Trump is thankfully absent from this book, as are any sort of explicit politics. In its place we have the platonic ideal of the interview with the Rust Belt Trump voter that took over political journalism in late 2016/early 2017.


Alain de Bouton's School of Life videos on YouTube are engaging, but often shallow and pretentious. This book was better. It did not change my life, but it made Proust accessible and made me rethink how I view friendship and the communities I live in.


I had Sayntani Dasgupta in a class and can confirm that she is just as funny and thoughtful in person as she is in this book. This collection of essays explores contrasts between life in India and life in the United States with insightful wit. I love when she describes streets in the US as "shampooed" when compared with the roads in New Delhi.



I'll always roll my eyes at writers who insist their names be lowercase (that includes bell hooks). But danah boyd offers a smart, nuanced look at how teenagers use social media. She cuts through parental bogeymen and media hype to show how Instagram of the 2010s is the mall of the 1980s.


This book is interactive. It draws a distinction between fast, instinctive, subconscious thought and slow, reflective, conscious thought, and puts the reader in situations where the strengths and weaknesses of the two systems are made intuitive. Lots of fun.


It’s hard to find science writing that's meatier than pop science but doesn't feel like homework. I think Greene nails it here. His use of accessible analogies and detailed footnotes makes the book easy to pick up without being too hand-wavey.



Too deep for me. I liked the part about the “noble lie” though. It made me think about how/why we build inviolable bedrocks of self-worth with stuff like unconditional love or racial superiority.


I love that Snicket doesn’t shy away from showing death. He says losing someone is like walking up some stairs, expecting one more step, and then feeling a jolt when your foot keeps falling. I don't think many children’s novels address death as honestly and sensitively as this one.


Philip Gourevitch is a good reporter and strong writer. Unfortunately, he fails to place the Rwandan genocide in context. Without a good anthropological/historical grounding, the book becomes a long gawk at depravity.



Barack Obama wrote this before getting into politics, and it's written with a level of candor you probably wouldn't see from someone on the campaign trail (though I'm sure he always had big ambitions). The prose can get grandiose at times, but his discussions of identity and inheritance are thoughtful and usually unpretentious. You would want this guy to become president after reading this book.


This Newberry Award winning book is about a road trip to Lewiston, Idaho. I went to college about 40 minutes away from Lewiston and was surprised that someone would write a book about such an unremarkable part of of the country. The author's from Cleveland so she knows how to find good stories in boring places.


This is the most realistic representation of high school I’ve seen in fiction. It follows a young football star’s changing priorities as he navigates his junior year. The problems he runs into, especially in family life, are ordinary but explored with enough subtlety to be gripping.



Set in the Depression-Era Midwest, this children’s novel deals with some heavy topics. Bud Caldwell escapes an abusive foster home and goes in search of a famous musician he suspects is his father. Bud is funny, scrappy, and a great lens into both the racial and economic turmoil and the jazzy ambience of that period of American history.


Personality is a huge aspect of identity, as important as race or gender. Susan Cain describes the strengths of introverts, and how they can often be crowded out in our extrovert-friendly culture. She is exhaustive in her research and practical in her advice.


Having survived the Holocaust, the psychiatrist Victor Frankl understood the depths of human suffering. This book is a study of how he was able to find purpose after having everything he knew taken away from him. I like that he doesn't present a universal idea of what meaning is, but rather a process for seeking it out.



D. Watkins went from selling crack to a Master's degree at Johns Hopkins and a professorship at the University of Baltimore. This collection of essays describes a life on the violent, east side ("Beast Side") of Baltimore unimaginable to most Americans. He gets a bit repetitive and his politics are a bit too radical for me, but his perspective is one that rarely (though more so recently) makes it into the public discourse.


J.L. Chestnut was the first black attorney in Selma, Alabama. His memoir shows the fear, violence, and jubilation of living in the heart of the Jim Crow South during the Civil Rights Movement. Particularly interesting to me were the descriptions of racial discord in Selma in the 80s and 90s, showing the continuing struggle decades after Bloody Sunday.


This novel is very much a product of its 1999 release year, centering on a murder during the height of the Satanic Panic. Daniel Handler (who also wrote A Series of Unfortunate Events under the name "Lemony Snicket") seems to specialize in writing young characters straining toward adulthood. The high school students here are witty, thoughtful, and written with care.



Pretty much everything you might see in an undergraduate math degree shows up in this book. Steven Strogatz presents it all with a flair for story-telling. He's a great communicator, keeping a nice balance between accessibility and informativeness.
